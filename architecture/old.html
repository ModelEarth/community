Mass Timber Warehouse with Stacked Container Front



Structural Mass Timber - Laminated Veneer Lumber (LVL) is no more costly than steel, while providing ease of construction, improved performance, and longterm carbon capture while expanding markets for Georgia forestry products. "Mass timber gains its Flash Flame Resistance (FFR) rating [which exceeds steel] by charring slightly during exposure to fire. This charring insulates the interior sections from further damage for a considerably long period of time." - John Bleasby

Design Considerations
Could structures be located in corners of property to create density clusters with adjacent property owners?
Are there advantages to using smaller gasifiers that are transferable in shipping containers?
Should portions of the interior use nanowood shielding to protect timber from chemicals, moisture and heat?

Roof and Water Retention
Can the angle of the south facing roof be 30° to optimize for solar? (Better yet, 31.1° matching the latitude.)
Can the roof's weather seal use the body of the solar panels to avoid additional weight and roofing expense?
In addtion to water collected from the roof, will surrounding surace water be retained and put to use?

Along the exterior of the south side of the building, a row of stacked high-cube containers (each with 9.5' tall interiors) could provide portable production space and growing areas. Sets of containers could be connected to create open workareas. A flat roof couild extend over the containers as a buttress for the angled solar roof. The area where the angled roof meets the side roof could direct rainwater to tanks at either end of the facility.

Shipping containers could be built onsite from compressed superwood produced at the site using heat from waste gasification. Production of superwood requires a temperature of 100º celsius to create new hydrogen bonds while compressing wood in boiling hydrogen peroxide. About superwood

Superwood Products and Controlled Environment Agriculture (CEA)
Freight containers produced from heat-treated superwood generate lignin for use in the production of activated carbon and other bioproducts.

Carbon-enriched soil and nutrient fluids can be used to grow plants during shipment in superwood freight containers built onsite.

• Bullet-proof superwood is fire resistant as a result of the increased density and reduced porosity.

• Safe bio-based flame retardant additives can also be added prior to the compression of superwood and transparent wood.

An Activated Carbon Study in 2016 found lignin provides a coconut-like activated carbon structure that is two to three times more valuable than activated carbon from wood.
more Like coconut, lignin has tighter graphitic platelet spacings to remove more small molecules from drinking water. Activated carbon made from lignin is softer than from coconut shells, which allows it to be used as a powder for faster absorption of contaminants, with less water-leaching ash than present powdered activated carbons.
